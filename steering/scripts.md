| `tournament` | Tournament-style adversarial review. **Round 1:** @dark-architect loads `grill-me` skill and uses its branch-by-branch questioning method to produce N numbered challenges rated FATAL/SERIOUS/MINOR. **If multiple defense angles exist for a challenge, spawn ALL of them in parallel (2-5 defenders per challenge). Only spawn 1 defender if there's genuinely only one viable counter-argument — and state why.** **Round 2:** For each STRONG challenge (SERIOUS+), spawn a defender agent (usually @architect) to counter-argue. Prune MINOR challenges immediately. **Round 3:** @dark-architect reviews defenses, kills weak ones, escalates surviving concerns. **Stop when:** all challenges are either killed (idea is solid) or undefeated (real risk found). Output: final verdict with surviving risks and recommended changes. Best for: architecture decisions, new infrastructure proposals, migration strategies, greenfield design validation. **NEVER skip Round 3.** The orchestrator does not declare winners — only the Dark Architect can kill or accept a defense. **Fact-check rule:** If the Dark Architect rejects a defense based on a technical claim about how a tool/library/framework works (not a design opinion), the orchestrator must verify the claim before accepting the rejection. Unverified technical claims cannot kill a defense. |
| `deathmatch` | Extended tournament with redemption round. Like `tournament` but WOUNDED ideas get a second chance. **Round 1:** @dark-architect loads `grill-me` skill and uses its branch-by-branch questioning method to produce N numbered challenges rated FATAL/SERIOUS/MINOR. **Round 2:** Defenders counter-argue SERIOUS+ challenges. **Round 3:** @dark-architect declares SURVIVED or WOUNDED (not killed — wounded). **Round 4 (DEATHMATCH):** Each WOUNDED challenge gets ONE redemption attempt. New arguments only — can't repeat failed defenses. Can reframe, cite evidence, propose new mitigations, or concede but argue the system works despite the flaw. Survivors from Round 3 get stress-tested (can DA find a deeper flaw?). **Round 5 (FINAL VERDICT):** DA rules on deathmatch attempts: REDEEMED (found genuine value despite wound) or DEAD (no redemption). Survivors get final check: CONFIRMED SURVIVOR or LATE WOUND (deeper flaw found). Output: full scoreboard with final status per challenge + mandatory pre-deployment actions. Best for: complex systems with many moving parts where you want maximum scrutiny but don't want good ideas killed prematurely. |
| `wargame` | Full-spectrum decision analysis that models BOTH cooperative and adversarial outcomes. Unlike `deathmatch` (which only attacks), `wargame` maps all players' incentives and finds the path of least resistance before stress-testing it. **Phase 1 (INTEL):** Identify all parties involved. For each party: what do they want, what do they fear, what's their best alternative if no deal? Map where interests align and where they conflict. **Phase 2 (DIPLOMACY):** Design the cooperative path — the deal where everyone says yes. Find the win-win. Sequence actions from easiest (voluntary agreement) to hardest (legal escalation). For each step, model the probability of cooperation based on incentive alignment. **Phase 3 (DEATHMATCH):** Attack the cooperative path. What if Party X refuses? What if they lie? What if they stall? For each refusal point, define the escalation move. Rate each refusal scenario by likelihood (LIKELY/POSSIBLE/UNLIKELY) and severity (BLOCKING/DELAYING/MINOR). **Phase 4 (ESCALATION LADDER):** Produce a decision tree. Start with diplomacy (cheapest, fastest). At each "no" branch, escalate to the next level. Each level has: trigger condition (what "no" looks like), escalation action, cost, timeline, and fallback if THIS level also fails. **Output:** A sequenced game plan that tries cooperation first, has pre-planned responses to every refusal, and only reaches adversarial measures as a last resort. Includes: incentive map, cooperation probability per party, decision tree with branches, and "walk away" threshold (when to stop and accept the loss). Best for: multi-party situations, negotiations, disputes, business deals, any decision where other humans' choices affect your outcome. |
